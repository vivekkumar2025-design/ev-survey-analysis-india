# Summary & Conclusions: EV Survey and Analysis Project

**Author:** Vivek Kumar  
**Date:** June 2026

---

## What This Project Was About

In March 2024, I conducted a 51-respondent survey for a university assignment on whether Electric Vehicles help combat climate change. The survey produced some clear patterns, some expected results, and a few findings that seemed obvious at the time but turned out to be more predictive than I realised.

Two years later, with real data from India's EV market, I came back to those survey responses and asked a simple question: Were the people right?

The answer is largely yes — which says something useful about survey research when it is done honestly and its limitations are acknowledged upfront.

---

## What the Survey Got Right

**The barriers people identified in 2024 are exactly the barriers that defined India's EV story in 2025 and 2026.**

High cost remained the top reason people did not buy personal EVs. Charging infrastructure was called out by 45.1% of respondents — and Delhi ended up with a deficit of over 27,000 charging points against its own requirement. Long charging time was flagged by 43.1% of respondents, and as of 2026, fast charging outside major urban corridors is still scarce.

The only major barrier our survey missed was battery degradation — the gradual loss of battery capacity that early EV owners in Indian climate conditions are now experiencing. This was not widely discussed in 2024. It is a significant issue in 2026.

---

## What the Survey Got Wrong (Or Overstated)

**72.6% of respondents believed EVs strongly help combat climate change. The reality is more complicated.**

Because India's electricity grid still depends heavily on coal, a battery electric car in India saves approximately 20% fewer lifecycle CO2 emissions than a petrol vehicle — not the 60-70% saving you would see in a country like France with a cleaner grid. EVs are better for the climate in India, but not dramatically so, not yet. The environmental case strengthens every year as India adds more renewable energy capacity.

**88% of respondents said their future travel preference is electric. Most of them have not yet switched.**

The intention-action gap in India's EV market is real. Demand and aspiration are not the bottleneck. The bottlenecks are exactly what the other survey questions identified: cost, infrastructure, and trust in product quality. When Ola Electric — at the time India's top EV brand — received 10,644 government-level complaints in a single year and saw its sales drop 51% the following year, it demonstrated how quickly trust can collapse when the product and service do not meet expectations.

---

## The Three Things India Needs to Get Right

Based on the survey data, the national trend data, and two years of watching this sector:

**1. Charging infrastructure has to come before demand, not after.**
India cannot wait for EV adoption to reach a threshold before building chargers. The chargers need to be there for the adoption to happen. Delhi's deficit of 27,000 charging points is not a future planning problem — it is a present barrier to adoption. The Delhi EV Policy 2026 with its 7,000 new charging points planned for this year alone is a step in the right direction, but implementation track record matters as much as policy ambition.

**2. Product quality and service network must scale with sales.**
The Ola Electric story is the most important case study in India's EV sector. The lesson is not that Ola's technology was bad — it is that the company scaled sales faster than it built service capacity, and the result was tens of thousands of unhappy customers and a 51% sales decline. The brands that are winning in 2025-26 — TVS, Bajaj, Ather, Tata — are the ones that invested in service before chasing volume.

**3. The environmental argument needs to be honest.**
EVs are better than petrol vehicles in India today, but the full environmental benefit depends on cleaning up the electricity grid. India added 44 GW of solar and 6 GW of wind in FY2026 alone. As that renewable capacity grows, every EV on Indian roads becomes progressively greener without any change to the vehicle itself. The environmental case for EVs in India is real and growing — it just requires honesty about where it stands today versus where it will stand in five years.

---

## What I Learned Doing This

Doing a survey with 51 respondents and then returning to that data two years later with real-world information to check against was more instructive than doing either exercise alone.

Survey data is only useful if you are honest about its limitations — sample size, demographic skew, and the fact that stated preferences are not the same as actual behaviour. Our survey told us what urban educated youth in Delhi thought about EVs in March 2024. It did not tell us what rural India thought, or what people who had actually owned an EV for three years thought, or what the second-order effects of battery degradation would look like.

At the same time, the directional accuracy of what our respondents identified as barriers — cost, infrastructure, charging time — was high. People who think carefully about a problem, even without technical expertise, can identify the real constraints.

The gap between what people said they wanted (88% saying they prefer electric travel in the future) and what they actually did (only 8.27% of vehicle sales being electric in FY2026) is the most interesting finding this project produced. That gap is not about preference or awareness. It is structural. Fix the infrastructure. Fix the cost. Fix the service. The demand is already there.

---

## Final Note on Honesty

This project is based on a survey of 51 people, which is a small sample. The national analysis draws from publicly available data, cited in full. Where I did not have data, I said so. Where the data told an inconvenient story — like the 20% lifecycle CO2 saving figure — I included it anyway.

I think honest research, even from a small student project, is more useful than inflated claims. Employers and researchers can work with honest data. They cannot work with numbers that were made to look better than they are.
