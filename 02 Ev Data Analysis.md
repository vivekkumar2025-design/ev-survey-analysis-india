## Section 1 — Survey Data Analysis (Primary Research, March 2024)

This section covers what our survey of 51 respondents revealed in March 2024, with commentary on how those responses compare to what actually happened in India's EV sector over the next two years.

### 1.2 EV Usage: Have Respondents Actually Used an EV?

| Response | Count | Percentage |
|----------|-------|------------|
| Yes      | 15    | 29.4%      |
| No       | 36    | 70.6%      |

**Chart Description:** A pie chart of this data shows roughly a 70/30 split, with the "No" segment being more than twice the size of the "Yes" segment.

**What this tells us:** In March 2024, roughly 7 in 10 respondents had never actually used an electric vehicle. This is important context for interpreting all other survey responses — most opinions came from people who had not experienced EVs firsthand. Their perceptions were shaped by news coverage, social media, and word of mouth, not personal experience.

**2026 Update:** India's EV penetration reached 8.27% of all vehicle sales in FY2026. The number of people who have personally used an EV has grown substantially, particularly in urban areas. An updated survey today would likely show a much higher "Yes" response, especially among the 20–25 age group.

| Category                        | Count | Percentage |
|---------------------------------|-------|------------|

| Personal Vehicle                | 21    | 41.2%      |
| Online Platform (Electric Cabs) | 11    | 21.6%      |

*(Note: Multiple selections were allowed, so percentages exceed 100%)*

**Chart Description:** A horizontal bar chart shows Public Vehicle as the clear leader at 64.7%, followed by Personal Vehicle at 41.2%, Electric Cabs at 21.6%, and None at 5.9%.

**Key Insight:** Most respondents preferred EVs in a public or shared context rather than as personal ownership. This aligns with economic reality — EVs have a higher upfront cost, and most urban youth in India rely on public transport or ride-hailing for daily commuting.

**2026 Update:** This is where India has actually made its most progress. Electric three-wheelers and e-rickshaws now make up approximately 35% of all EV sales. E-buses are being inducted under PM E-Drive. The preference people expressed in 2024 for public EV usage has been validated by the market.

### 1.4 Purpose of Using EVs

| Reason              | Count | Percentage |
|---------------------|-------|------------|
| Environment Concern | 29    | 56.9%      |
| Availability        | 23    | 45.1%      |
| Cost Effectiveness  | 23    | 45.1%      |
| Others              | 6     | 11.8%      |

**Chart Description:** Horizontal bar chart. Environment concern leads but availability and cost are nearly equal and together represent a strong practical motivation.

**Key Insight:** Environmental concern is the top stated reason, but the gap between environment (56.9%) and practical factors like availability and cost (45.1% each) is smaller than one might expect. People are motivated by both values and economics, roughly equally.

**What this means for policy:** Subsidies work because they address the cost barrier. But if the product is not available where people live, subsidies alone will not drive adoption. This explains why EV adoption remains concentrated in a handful of states — Uttar Pradesh, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, and Delhi account for about 50% of all EV sales.

### 1.5 Future Travel Preference

| Preference                  | Percentage |
|-----------------------------|------------|
| Private EV                  | 49.0%      |
| Public EV                   | 39.2%      |
| Private Conventional Vehicle| 2.0%       |
| Public Conventional Vehicle | 9.8%       |

**Chart Description:** A pie chart showing Private EV as the largest segment at 49%, followed by Public EV at 39.2%, Public Conventional at 9.8%, and Private Conventional at just 2%.

**Key Insight:** Nearly 90% of respondents (combined Private EV + Public EV) said their future preference is electric. Only 12% said they would continue using conventional vehicles. This is the most striking finding in the survey. In 2024, most people had never used an EV — yet almost all of them said they wanted to switch to one.

This gap between current behaviour and future intent is the central story of India's EV market. The demand is there. The constraint is on the supply side — availability, affordability, and infrastructure.

### 1.6 Why People Do Not Buy Personal EVs

| Barrier                | Count | Percentage |
|------------------------|-------|------------|
| High Cost              | 25    | 49.0%      |
| EV Infrastructure      | 23    | 45.1%      |
| Long Charging Time     | 22    | 43.1%      |
| Short Range            | 18    | 35.3%      |

**Chart Description:** Horizontal bar chart with all four bars close together, indicating that no single barrier dominates — all four are significant.

**2026 Validation — How Accurate Were These Concerns?**

- **High Cost:** Still accurate. EV four-wheelers are 20–40% more expensive than equivalent petrol vehicles upfront. Battery replacement costs remain high.
- **EV Infrastructure:** Completely validated. Delhi has 8,849 charging points against a requirement of 36,150. India has roughly one public charger per 235 EVs. 18% of installed chargers are non-functional.
- **Long Charging Time:** Still a real issue for four-wheelers. Fast chargers exist but are limited to major corridors and urban centres. Most home charging is overnight, which works for daily commuters but limits flexibility.
- **Short Range:** Has improved with newer models. Ather Energy launched next-generation two-wheelers with improved range in June 2025. But for four-wheelers in the budget segment, range anxiety is still real.

**Conclusion:** Our respondents correctly identified all four major barriers in 2024. Two years later, infrastructure and charging time remain the biggest unresolved problems in India's EV transition.

### 1.7 How India Can Transition Completely to EVs

| Factor                      | Percentage |
|-----------------------------|------------|
| Government Subsidy          | 41.2%      |
| EV Infrastructure           | 31.4%      |
| Strict Laws on ICE Vehicles | 11.8%      |
| Public Perception Change    | 15.7%      |

**Chart Description:** A pie chart with Government Subsidy as the largest segment, followed by EV Infrastructure, then Public Perception and Stricter Laws as smaller slices.

**Analysis:** Respondents overwhelmingly pointed to government action — subsidy and infrastructure — as the path to full EV adoption. They were largely right. Private demand alone has not been sufficient to drive adoption. The FAME schemes, PM E-Drive, and now the Delhi EV Policy 2026 with its Rs. 3,954 crore outlay are exactly the kind of government-led push respondents identified as necessary.

| Estimate    | Percentage |
|-------------|------------|

| 70% – 80%   | 38.8%      |
| 80% – 90%   | 53.1%      |
| 90% – 100%  | 0%         |

**Chart Description:** Pie chart dominated by the 80–90% segment, with 70–80% as the second largest.

**Analysis:** Most respondents expected EVs to replace 70–90% of conventional vehicles. The national 2030 target of 30% EV penetration in private cars — which currently looks ambitious — suggests the 80–90% estimate for 2030 is too optimistic. However, for two-wheelers and three-wheelers, the 80% target is actually government policy. By segment, respondents may be more right than they seem.

### 1.9 Are EVs Useful for Climate Combat?

| Response        | Percentage |
|-----------------|------------|
| Strongly Agree  | 47.1%      |
| Agree           | 25.5%      |
| Neutral         | 13.7%      |
| Disagree        | 9.8%       |
| Strongly Disagree| 3.9%      |

**Chart Description:** Pie chart with nearly half the chart taken by "Strongly Agree" in one colour, followed by "Agree", then smaller slices for neutral and negative responses.

**Analysis:** 72.6% of respondents agree or strongly agree that EVs help combat climate change. 13.7% are unsure. Only 13.7% disagree or strongly disagree.

**What the Data Actually Says:** This is where the survey data intersects with a more complex truth. EVs in India, given the current coal-heavy electricity grid, save approximately 20% fewer lifecycle CO2 emissions compared to ICE vehicles (IEA, 2024). They are better — but not dramatically so, not yet. The optimism of respondents is directionally correct but overstated. EVs will become significantly better for the climate as India's grid becomes cleaner. Between 2020 and 2024, EV adoption prevented approximately 10 million tonnes of CO2 emissions in India (WRI India, 2024) — a real number, but a small fraction of India's total emissions.

## Section 2 — National Trend Analysis (Secondary Data, 2022–2026)

### 2.1 India EV Sales Growth: 2022–2026

| Year    | Total EV Sales  | YoY Growth | EV Market Share |
|---------|-----------------|------------|-----------------|
| 2022    | ~0.7 million    | —          | ~2.5%           |
| 2023    | ~1.6 million    | ~128%      | ~5.5%           |
| 2024    | ~1.95 million   | ~24%       | ~7%             |
| 2025    | ~2.27 million   | ~16.3%     | ~8%             |
| 2026*   | ~2.7 million    | ~19%       | ~8.5%           |

*2026 figure is trailing 12-month (June 2025 to May 2026) per VAHAN Dashboard

**Chart Description:** A line chart of this data would show steep exponential growth from 2022 to 2023, followed by a moderation into consistent 16–20% annual growth from 2024 onward. The curve is flattening — which is normal market maturation — but still pointing upward.

**Key Observation:** Growth is real and consistent. But the rate is slowing. The massive early jumps came from a very low base. Sustaining 16–20% growth while the base gets larger requires solving the infrastructure and affordability problems that our 2024 survey already identified.

### 2.2 Segment Breakdown: Who Is Actually Buying EVs?

| Segment           | FY2026 Sales | Share of Total |
|-------------------|--------------|----------------|
| Electric 2-Wheelers | ~1.28 million | 56.4%        |
| Electric 3-Wheelers | ~0.83 million | 34–35%       |
| Electric 4-Wheelers | ~1.76 lakh   | ~7%           |
| E-Buses / Others  | Small         | <3%            |

**Chart Description:** A pie chart of this data shows 2-wheelers and 3-wheelers together accounting for over 90% of India's EV market. 4-wheelers, which dominate EV conversations in media, are a small fraction of actual sales.

**Key Insight:** India's EV revolution is happening on two wheels and three wheels, not four. E-rickshaws and electric scooters are the real story. This matters because the charging infrastructure debate is largely about four-wheelers — two-wheelers and e-rickshaws primarily charge at home or through battery swapping, which requires far less public infrastructure.

| Public Charging Points    | 8,849         | 36,150      | -27,301  |

**Chart Description:** A grouped bar chart comparing "Current" vs "Required" for charging points and swapping stations would show the requirement bars roughly 4x taller than the current bars — a stark visual gap.

**Key Insight:** Delhi, with its relatively high income levels, strong government commitment, and urbanised population, has the best EV policy in India and still has a charging infrastructure deficit of over 27,000 points. The rest of the country is in a worse position. Infrastructure is not lagging slightly — it is lagging catastrophically relative to the EV adoption targets.

### 2.4 The Environmental Impact: What EVs Are Actually Doing

| Metric                                  | Value                          |
|-----------------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| CO2 saved by Indian EVs (2020–2024)     | ~10 million tonnes (WRI India) |
| Lifecycle CO2 saving vs ICE (India)     | ~20% (IEA, 2024)              |
| Lifecycle CO2 saving vs ICE (France)    | ~60–70%                        |
| India's vehicular contribution to PM2.5 in Delhi | ~39%                  |
| India's EV penetration in FY2026        | 8.27% of all vehicle sales    |

**Key Insight:** The 20% lifecycle CO2 saving figure is honest and important. It means EVs in India are not the silver bullet that 72% of survey respondents believed them to be — not yet. The full environmental benefit depends on India cleaning up its electricity grid. As solar and wind capacity grows (India added 44 GW solar + 6 GW wind in FY2026), the same EVs will become progressively cleaner every year.

The local air quality benefit is more immediate. Zero tailpipe emissions in Delhi streets — where vehicular pollution is the single largest source of PM2.5 — has measurable health benefits even if the lifecycle CO2 saving is modest.

### 2.5 The Ola Electric Case Study: What Happens When You Scale Too Fast

| Metric                                    | Value                             |
|-------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Ola Electric complaints (Sep 2023–Aug 2024)| 10,644 (government level alone)  |
| Monthly complaints (National Consumer Helpline) | ~80,000                    |
| YoY sales change (2025)                   | -51% decline                      |
| Market position change                    | #1 in 2024 → #4 in 2025          |
| Regulatory action                         | CCPA Show Cause Notice (Oct 2024)|

**Chart Description:** A bar chart comparing Ola's market share in 2024 vs 2025 alongside TVS, Bajaj, and Ather would show a dramatic inversion — Ola going from the top bar to below three competitors.

**Key Insight:** This is the clearest case study in India's EV sector of what happens when sales outpace service capacity and product quality. HSBC analysts visiting Ola service centers in 2024 found them overwhelmed, understaffed, and unable to maintain basic service standards. The lesson for the broader market: EV adoption is not just about selling vehicles — it requires a service ecosystem that the company builds before, not after, scaling sales volume.

## Note on Methodology and Limitations

This analysis combines primary survey data (51 respondents, March 2024) with secondary data from publicly available sources including VAHAN Dashboard, EVreporter, JMK Research, IEA Global EV Outlook 2024, WRI India, and government policy documents.

The survey sample is small and skewed toward urban educated youth. It should not be treated as nationally representative. The value of the survey data is not in its statistical generalisability but in its directional accuracy — the concerns respondents raised in 2024 mapped closely onto the actual problems that emerged in the sector over the following two years.
